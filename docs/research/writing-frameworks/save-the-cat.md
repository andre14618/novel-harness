# Save the Cat — Framework Report

Research target: extract structural rules and constraints from Blake Snyder's *Save the Cat!* (screenwriting, 2005) and Jessica Brody's novel adaptation *Save the Cat! Writes a Novel* (Ten Speed Press, 2018) that can be encoded as planner constraints, beat prompts, or checker rules in a programmatic novel-writing harness.

Date: 2026-04-29
Author lineage: Blake Snyder (originator); Jessica Brody (novel adaptation, *SCWN*)

---

## 1. Framework summary

**Origin.** Blake Snyder, a working Hollywood spec-script writer, codified his beat sheet and the ten "story types" in *Save the Cat!: The Last Book on Screenwriting You'll Ever Need* (Michael Wiese Productions, 2005). The book's title is itself a craft prescription: the protagonist must do something — literally or figuratively save a cat — early in the story so that the reader/viewer roots for them.

Jessica Brody, a novelist of 20+ titles and founder of Writing Mastery Academy, adapted the framework for prose in *Save the Cat! Writes a Novel* (Ten Speed Press, 2018, ISBN 978-0-399-57974-5). She rewrote the percentages around prose pacing rather than 110-page screenplays, expanded the genres with novel-specific examples, and added the Transformation Machine framing.

**Core thesis.** Successful stories share a deep narrative skeleton: 15 named beats fired at predictable percentages of the story length, each with a specific dramatic mission, organized around a flawed protagonist's transformation arc. A story's "genre" (in Snyder's idiosyncratic sense) is not its setting/marketing label but its dramatic shape — there are exactly ten — and each shape requires three specific structural ingredients.

**Problem solved.** Diagnoses a *broken* story by checking which beat is missing or misplaced. Provides a deterministic outline lattice that can be filled before drafting begins. Forces the writer to plant the internal-transformation mechanism (theme, B-story, shard of glass) early so it can fire at the climax.

## 2. Concept-phase prescriptions

### 2.1 The logline formula

Snyder's canonical logline formula (the "mini-story" version cited by *Save the Cat!*):

> "On the verge of a Stasis=Death moment, a flawed protagonist Breaks into Two; but when the Midpoint happens, he/she must learn the Theme Stated, before All Is Lost."

Extended (with antagonist):

> "On the verge of a Stasis=Death moment, a flawed protagonist has a Catalyst and Breaks Into Two with the B Story; but when the Midpoint happens, he/she must learn the Theme Stated, before the All Is Lost, to defeat (or stop) the flawed antagonist (from getting away with his/her plan)."

Snyder also provides a four-part one-sentence logline test: irony, a compelling mental picture, audience and cost, and a killer title. A logline that lacks irony fails the test.

### 2.2 The ten Save the Cat story genres

Snyder/Brody insist a writer pick exactly one. Each requires THREE structural ingredients (the "ingredients" or "essential elements"):

| # | Genre | Ingredient 1 | Ingredient 2 | Ingredient 3 |
|---|-------|--------------|--------------|--------------|
| 1 | Monster in the House | Monster (supernatural or insane) | House (confined space) | Sin (transgression that summoned monster) |
| 2 | Golden Fleece | Road (measurable journey) | Team/Buddy | Prize (essential destination/object) |
| 3 | Out of the Bottle | Deserving hero | Magic with rules | Lesson (live without magic) |
| 4 | Dude with a Problem | Innocent hero | Sudden event | Life-or-death battle |
| 5 | Rites of Passage | Life problem (universal) | Wrong way (avoidance) | Acceptance (must change self) |
| 6 | Buddy Love | Incomplete hero | Counterpart who completes them | Complication keeping them apart |
| 7 | Whydunit | Detective | Secret (about human nature) | Dark turn (detective compromised) |
| 8 | The Fool Triumphant | Fool (underestimated) | Establishment (opposing force) | Transmutation |
| 9 | Institutionalized | Group (family/org) | Choice (join/leave/destroy) | Sacrifice |
| 10 | Superhero | Power (special skill) | Nemesis (equal/superior) | Curse (relatability cost) |

Brody notes Genre selection is a *front-loaded* decision: it pre-determines the shape of every beat. A "Buddy Love" novel where the catalyst is a monster invasion will fail because the wrong dramatic engine is firing.

### 2.3 The Transformation Machine

Brody's expansion. Three concept-phase character variables:

- **Wound (Shard of Glass)** — a buried injury or absorbed wrong from the protagonist's past. The story's job is to surface it and force the protagonist to face it. The Shard is "a sharp-edged incident, bad behavior, tough truth or wrong done and absorbed that the hero swallowed a long time ago. Skin has grown up around its hard corners."
- **Want** — what the protagonist *thinks* will solve their problem (drives the external A-story).
- **Need** — what the protagonist actually requires (revealed by the B-story and theme).

The "Six Things That Need Fixing" — Snyder's term for a laundry list of minor flaws, enemies, rivals, deficits established in the Setup that get "exploded later in the script, turned on their heads and cured." Six is arbitrary; the principle is *front-load the deficits so they can be paid off.*

### 2.4 "Save the Cat" moment

A scene early in the story (typically inside the Setup, before the Catalyst at 10%) where the protagonist does something that proves they deserve our sympathy — saves the cat, defends the underdog, shows hidden kindness. Without it, an unsympathetic protagonist loses the reader.

### 2.5 Concept-phase checklist (Brody)

A premise should answer in ~one sentence:
1. Who is the flawed hero? (and what is the flaw)
2. What is their Stasis=Death — the place they're stuck?
3. What event disrupts it (Catalyst)?
4. What new world do they enter (Act 2)?
5. What do they need to learn (Theme)?

If any answer is missing, the premise is not ready for outlining.

## 3. Planning-phase prescriptions — the 15-beat sheet

Brody's percentages (from *SCWN*, the Beat Sheet Calculator, and the official Save the Cat resources). All percentages are of total novel word count.

| # | Beat | Pct | Function | STRUCTURAL or OPTIONAL |
|---|------|-----|----------|------------------------|
| 1 | Opening Image | 0–1% | "Before" snapshot of the flawed hero in their stuck world. Must visually/emotionally register the status quo. | STRUCTURAL |
| 2 | Theme Stated | ~5% | Another character (often the B-story character) speaks the lesson the hero must learn. Hero typically dismisses or ignores it. | STRUCTURAL |
| 3 | Setup | 1–10% | Establishes the hero's world, supporting cast, and the "Six Things That Need Fixing" — the deficits to be paid off later. Includes the Save-the-Cat moment. | STRUCTURAL |
| 4 | Catalyst | ~10% | World-disrupting external event that makes return to the status quo impossible. Sometimes called the inciting incident. | STRUCTURAL |
| 5 | Debate | 10–20% | Hero hesitates: should I go? Reveals stakes and the cost of action. | STRUCTURAL |
| 6 | Break Into 2 | ~20% (Brody also writes ~30% in the prose version with longer Setups) | Hero proactively chooses to enter the new world / accept the mission. Crossing the threshold. | STRUCTURAL |
| 7 | B Story | ~22% | Introduction of the relationship/character that carries the theme. Often a love interest, mentor, sibling, foil. | STRUCTURAL (if the story has a transformation arc) |
| 8 | Fun and Games | 20–50% | The "promise of the premise" — the action/adventure/comedy/romance that the cover/marketing copy promises. Reader's reward for buying the book. | STRUCTURAL |
| 9 | Midpoint | ~50% | Either a False Victory (high point that masks deepening problems) or a False Defeat (low point that masks unrecognized progress). Stakes raised; A-story and B-story collide for the first time. | STRUCTURAL |
| 10 | Bad Guys Close In | 50–75% | Pressure mounts. If Midpoint was False Victory, things deteriorate; if False Defeat, things appear to improve while the real threat builds. Internal flaws compound. | STRUCTURAL |
| 11 | All Is Lost | ~75% | Lowest external point. The "whiff of death" — literal or metaphorical death (mentor dies, dream dies, relationship ends, old self dies). | STRUCTURAL |
| 12 | Dark Night of the Soul | 75–80% | Hero processes the loss. Internal reckoning. Quiet, introspective, often paired with the B-story character. | STRUCTURAL |
| 13 | Break Into 3 | ~80% | Realization beat. Hero synthesizes A-story + B-story + theme and sees how to win. The "aha" moment. | STRUCTURAL |
| 14 | Finale | 80–99% | Hero applies the new wisdom. Often broken into five "F" sub-beats: Gathering the Team, Executing the Plan, High Tower Surprise, Dig Deep Down, Execution of New Plan. Antagonist is defeated and the Six Things are paid off. | STRUCTURAL |
| 15 | Final Image | 99–100% | "After" snapshot mirroring the Opening Image. Demonstrates permanent transformation. | STRUCTURAL |

### 3.1 Structural vs optional

**All 15 are structural in Snyder/Brody's prescriptive framework.** Brody is explicit: a beat sheet missing a beat is a story missing an organ.

That said, the *form* a beat takes is flexible:
- **B Story** is structural but its character can be plural (a found-family ensemble) or non-romantic.
- **Save-the-Cat moment** floats inside the Setup; the percentage is not fixed.
- **Theme Stated** can be visual/situational rather than dialogue.
- **Midpoint** is False Victory OR False Defeat — pick one and it determines the slope of "Bad Guys Close In."

### 3.2 Failure modes Brody/Snyder name

- **Missing Theme Stated** → the climax feels unearned because there's no internal arc to land.
- **B Story not introduced by ~22%** → the protagonist has no vehicle for change, climax becomes pure plot mechanics.
- **Fun and Games doesn't deliver the premise** → readers feel cheated; the cover sold a thing they didn't get.
- **Midpoint at the wrong percentage** → second act drags or ends abruptly. Brody flags 50% as the diagnostic checkpoint.
- **All Is Lost lacks "whiff of death"** → reader doesn't feel the stakes; finale reads as routine.
- **Final Image doesn't mirror Opening Image** → arc doesn't visibly close; reader senses something missing without being able to name it.

## 4. Drafting-phase prescriptions

Brody writes less about prose-level rules than Snyder, but several concrete prescriptions surface:

### 4.1 Scene-level rules

- **Every scene advances at least one of: A-story, B-story, theme, character revelation.** A scene that advances none is a candidate for cutting.
- **Every scene has a "wants/gets" structure.** Protagonist enters with a goal; scene resolves with goal achieved, denied, or transformed.
- **Opening lines/images carry weight.** The Opening Image is the most-stressed example: a single concrete sensory detail that reads as an emoji of the hero's emotional state.
- **Closing lines/images set up the next beat.** Each scene's last beat should generate forward pressure.

### 4.2 Dialogue prescriptions

- **Theme Stated must be spoken by someone other than the POV character.** The POV typically dismisses it; that dismissal is the point.
- **B Story conversations carry the internal arc.** A-story conversations carry the plot.
- **Midpoint has a "truth bomb" — often dialogue that reframes everything.**

### 4.3 Snyder's prose-level techniques (still useful in novels)

- **Pope in the Pool** — bury exposition under a visually arresting scene so the reader doesn't notice they're being told plot mechanics.
- **Double Bump** — if your hero is too passive to leave the ordinary world after one Catalyst, give them a second push. Do not give them three; that's structural failure.
- **Six Things That Need Fixing** — Setup must establish the laundry list. Finale must explicitly pay off each item.
- **Save the Cat moment** — protagonist does something humane within the first ~10% to bank reader sympathy.

### 4.4 Internal-state cadence

The Transformation Machine implies a per-beat internal-state checklist:

- Opening Image: Hero is *in denial* of the wound.
- Catalyst: Wound *touched* but not acknowledged.
- Break Into 2: Hero attacks the want, not the need.
- Midpoint: First glimpse that want ≠ need.
- All Is Lost: Wound is fully exposed.
- Dark Night: Hero *sits* with the wound.
- Break Into 3: Hero *accepts* the need.
- Finale: Hero acts *from* the need.
- Final Image: Wound integrated; new equilibrium.

## 5. Validation/checking prescriptions

Brody's *SCWN* lists the diagnostic patterns explicitly. Each is a checker candidate:

1. **No Save-the-Cat moment** → unsympathetic protagonist, reader doesn't bond. Detection: scan first 10% for any humane/admirable proactive action by POV.
2. **Theme not stated by 5%** → climax has no internal target. Detection: look for any other-character dialogue/observation framing a life lesson.
3. **B Story missing or absent past 25%** → no vehicle for transformation. Detection: identify which character first appears in 20–25% range and carries thematic conversations after.
4. **B Story doesn't intersect A Story** → subplot reads as filler. Detection: check whether B-story character is present at Midpoint and Break Into 3.
5. **Catalyst doesn't disrupt the world** → Debate becomes hollow because there's nothing to flee. Detection: compare protagonist's stated options before/after 10% mark.
6. **Fun and Games fails the premise** → Detection: verify whether the genre's central activity (the monster hunting in MITH, the journey in Golden Fleece, the courtship in Buddy Love) is rendered in scenes between 20–50%.
7. **Midpoint flat** → no false victory, no false defeat, no stakes raise. Detection: check whether protagonist's external situation visibly changes at ~50% AND whether emotional valence pivots.
8. **All Is Lost lacks whiff of death** → climax reads as anticlimax. Detection: scan ~75% for literal death, end of relationship, end of dream, or symbolic death imagery.
9. **Dark Night of the Soul too short or too long** → either the realization isn't earned or the pacing dies. Detection: 75–80% should be quiet/introspective, not action-heavy.
10. **Break Into 3 unmotivated** → realization comes from outside the story. Detection: check whether the "aha" connects explicitly to the Theme Stated and to events from the B-story.
11. **Finale doesn't pay off the Six Things That Need Fixing** → setup elements left dangling. Detection: enumerate items established in Setup, confirm each is referenced/resolved in Finale.
12. **Final Image doesn't mirror Opening** → transformation not visible. Detection: structural similarity check between first and last scenes (same setting? same activity? inverted emotional valence?).
13. **No Mirror Moment** (Brody's term for the Midpoint's introspective beat where the hero glimpses what they must become) → arc has no fulcrum.
14. **Wrong genre ingredients** → e.g., Monster in the House without a confined "House" (no claustrophobia), Golden Fleece without a measurable Road. Detection: per-genre ingredient checklist.

## 6. Programmatic levers for the harness

These map STC prescriptions to concrete planner constraints, beat-prompt additions, or checker rules. Each is written as if implementable next sprint.

### Lever 1 — Genre as a planner-level constraint

Add a `stcGenre` field to `SeedInput.directives` (one of the 10). Use it to:
- Reject seeds whose premise doesn't match the genre's three ingredients (LLM checker on concept output).
- Inject genre-specific scene templates into Fun and Games (e.g., MITH → 4 monster encounters; Golden Fleece → 3 road-trip waypoints).
- Add a `genreIngredientsPresent: boolean[3]` validator at end of planning.

### Lever 2 — 15-beat percentage lattice as planner constraint

Extend the planning-plotter's chapter-skeleton output with a `stcBeat` tag per chapter. Validate with a deterministic check:

```
CATALYST chapter must have cumulativeWords / totalWords ∈ [0.08, 0.12]
MIDPOINT chapter must have cumulativeWords / totalWords ∈ [0.45, 0.55]
ALL_IS_LOST chapter ∈ [0.72, 0.78]
BREAK_INTO_3 chapter ∈ [0.78, 0.83]
FINAL_IMAGE chapter ≥ 0.98
```

Add a `chapter-plan-checker` rule: if a STRUCTURAL beat is missing or out of band, flag and route to chapter-plan-reviser.

### Lever 3 — Logline gate at concept exit

Before promoting concept→planning, force the concept agent to emit a Snyder-formula logline (with all five fillable slots: Stasis=Death, Catalyst, Break Into Two character, Theme, Antagonist). LLM checker validates each slot is non-empty and non-tautological. Drop into `seed_json.directives.logline`.

### Lever 4 — Transformation Machine fields on character profiles

Force `character-agent` to emit `{wound, want, need}` for the protagonist (and ideally B-story character). Fail concept phase if any field is generic/missing. These become beat-prompt inputs for `beat-writer` so internal state tracks the arc.

### Lever 5 — "Six Things That Need Fixing" registry

Concept phase emits `sixThings: string[]` (deficits/flaws/enemies established in setup). Planning encodes them in chapters 1–2 (Setup). Validation phase runs a deterministic check at chapter 14+ (Finale): are all six referenced/resolved in prose?

This is a high-value checker because dangling setups are a common LLM failure: the planner introduces a rival who never reappears.

### Lever 6 — Save-the-Cat moment as beat-level constraint

In any chapter tagged `setup` or `opening_image`, inject into the beat-writer prompt: "The POV character must perform one humane, proactive, sympathy-banking action in this beat (literal or figurative 'save the cat')." Add a checker: scan first 10% of prose for evidence of a proactive humane action by POV. Fire if absent.

### Lever 7 — Theme Stated as a planner-required beat

Planning must emit exactly one beat in the 4–7% range tagged `theme_stated` containing fields `{theme: string, speaker: string}`. Constraint: `speaker !== POV`. Add a checker: at the climax (Break Into 3), the POV's realization must reference or echo the same `theme` string (loose semantic check, LLM-judged).

### Lever 8 — B Story intersection check

`character-agent` outputs a `bStoryCharacter` field. Planning enforces: this character first appears in chapters covering 20–25% of word count, AND appears in Midpoint chapter, AND appears in Dark Night chapter, AND is present at Break Into 3. Failure → planner-reviser.

### Lever 9 — Midpoint type tag and cross-half consistency

Each plan declares `midpoint: { type: "false_victory" | "false_defeat", description: string }`. The "Bad Guys Close In" chapters (50–75%) must show monotonic deterioration if `false_victory`, or improvement masking deeper threat if `false_defeat`. Checker compares emotional valence vector across the second-act-2 chapters.

### Lever 10 — All-Is-Lost "whiff of death" detector

Per-beat checker for the chapter at 75%: does the prose contain death imagery, end-of-relationship language, mentor/ally loss, or symbolic-death cues? Implement as a small prompted classifier on the chapter's prose. Fire `missing_whiff_of_death` issue if absent → targeted rewrite.

### Lever 11 — Final Image mirror check

LLM-judged comparison of Opening Image and Final Image scenes: same setting? Same activity? Inverted emotional valence? Output a similarity-with-inversion score; fail below threshold. This is a single LLM call at the validation phase and would run cleanly on a 4B base.

### Lever 12 — Dark Night pacing constraint

Beat floor for Dark Night chapter: introspection density (interior thought / dialogue / sensory ratio) must exceed a threshold. Use existing `quality-detectors.ts` infrastructure to measure interiority and reject Dark Night chapters that read as action.

### Lever 13 — Genre-ingredient pre-write gate

When a chapter is tagged with a Fun-and-Games scene, inject the three genre ingredients into the beat-writer prompt as a constraint: "This is a Monster in the House story; this scene must take place inside [house], must show [monster] as a present threat, and must reference the original [sin] that summoned it."

### Lever 14 — Pope-in-the-Pool exposition packing

When a planner marks a chapter as exposition-heavy (lots of `establishedFacts` from world-state), rewrite-time inject Snyder's Pope-in-the-Pool guidance: "Exposition must be delivered while the POV is doing something visually/emotionally arresting. Do not deliver world-state facts in standalone paragraphs of explanation." Add a checker: percentage of words in info-dump paragraphs must be below threshold.

### Lever 15 — Double-Bump escalation rule

If the chapter-plan-reviser is invoked because the protagonist hasn't crossed into Act 2 by 25%, allow the reviser to insert exactly one additional Catalyst-class event (the "double bump"). Hard-cap at one. If the protagonist still won't cross at 30%, escalate to plan-assist (human gate) — this is structural failure.

## 7. Limitations / when STC fails

- **Formula homogenization.** The most cited critique (Slate 2013, Mythcreants, No Film School): when every story hits the same beats at the same percentages, audiences feel a subliminal sameness even on a first viewing. For a programmatic harness this is paradoxically *useful at small scales* (we want predictable structure for LLM-drafted novels) but catastrophic at scale (every harness-produced novel reads alike).
  - Mitigation: vary the *texture* inside beats, not the lattice. Genre selection + voice LoRA + character variables produce divergence inside an STC skeleton.

- **Doesn't fit all genres equally.** Daniel Calvisi (Film Courage) and others note STC works best for mainstream commercial three-act stories. Literary fiction, slice-of-life, mosaic narratives, and many literary-prestige forms *don't* follow this skeleton — and forcing them into it produces saccharine, market-genre prose. For a harness explicitly targeting commercial fantasy/LitRPG (per `project_fantasy_genre_focus`), STC is well-aligned. For literary aspiration, it's the wrong oracle.

- **Snyder's percentages are screenplay percentages.** Brody adjusted some (Break Into 2 in particular drifts from 20% to 30% in long-form prose because Setups breathe more in novels). The beat sheet calculator is the source of truth for prose; the screenwriting book's pages-based positions are NOT 1:1.

- **Mirror-moment / Save-the-Cat-moment dependencies on protagonist sympathy** assume a Western, single-POV, character-arc-driven story. Multi-POV epic fantasy (e.g., GRRM) and ensemble narratives violate the framework's bone structure — though even there, individual POV arcs can each be STC-shaped.

- **The "ten genres" classification is idiosyncratic.** "Institutionalized" doesn't map to bookstore shelves. The framework's genres are *dramatic engines*, not market categories — which makes them powerful for planning but confusing as user-facing labels. The harness should never expose them to end users without translation.

- **Theme Stated at 5% is sometimes too on-the-nose.** Literary readers notice. Brody acknowledges that "subtle" Theme Stated (a child's overheard remark, a graffiti wall, a song lyric) works better than dialogue.

- **The Six Things That Need Fixing is arbitrary in count.** Treat as "a list, not a count." The harness should not enforce literally six items.

```json
    "house": "string",
    "sin": "string"
  },
  "logline": {
    "stasisDeath": "string",
    "catalyst": "string",
    "breakIntoTwo": "string",
    "themeStated": "string",
    "allIsLost": "string",
    "antagonist": "string"
  },
  "transformationMachine": {
    "wound": "string",
    "want": "string",
    "need": "string"
  },
  "sixThingsToFix": ["string", "string", "..."],
```

```json

  "expectedPctMin": 0.08,
  "expectedPctMax": 0.12
```
