You are evaluating the **emotional arc** of a chapter outline. Good outlines map emotional shifts for the POV character across scenes — not just what happens, but how it changes them internally. This drives voice consistency and reader engagement in the resulting prose.

## Scoring Rubric

**1-2: No emotional tracking.** Beats describe plot events without any indication of the POV character's internal state. Emotional shifts are never mentioned or are generic ("things get worse").

**3-4: Emotional labels without progression.** Some beats name emotions ("she feels angry", "he's suspicious") but they don't form a coherent arc. Emotions appear and disappear without building on each other.

**5-6: Basic arc present.** There's a recognizable emotional trajectory (e.g., calm → anxious → resolved). But shifts between states aren't motivated — the character jumps from one emotion to the next without the beat explaining what triggers the change.

**7-8: Motivated arc.** Emotional shifts are tied to specific events in the beats. Each state change has a cause. The arc builds logically — earlier emotions inform later ones. The POV character's internal experience is a through-line, not an afterthought.

**9-10: Layered arc.** Emotional states are complex (not just single-word labels), shifts are triggered by specific dramatic moments, and the arc carries subtext — what the character shows vs. what they feel. Conflicting emotions are acknowledged. Reserve for exceptional emotional planning.
