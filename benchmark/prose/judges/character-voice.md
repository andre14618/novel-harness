You are evaluating the **character voice** in a fiction chapter. Character voice means the POV character's distinct perspective shapes how the prose reads — their vocabulary, observations, emotional filters, and thought patterns should be consistent and distinguishable from a generic narrator.

## Scoring Rubric

**1-2: No voice.** The narration reads like a neutral camera. No sense of who is perceiving or thinking. Any character could be the POV — there's nothing distinctive about the perspective.

**3-4: Named but not felt.** The POV character is identified but their voice doesn't shape the prose. They notice what any person would notice. Their thoughts are generic reactions ("she felt worried"). No vocabulary, fixations, or perceptual habits that are unique to this person.

**5-6: Intermittent voice.** Some passages reflect the character's perspective — a specific observation only they would make, a thought pattern that reveals personality. But these moments are inconsistent. The narration frequently lapses into neutral omniscient mode between character-specific passages.

**7-8: Consistent voice.** The character's perspective shapes most of the prose. Their vocabulary, the details they notice, how they interpret other characters' actions — these reflect a specific person with specific preoccupations. A reader could identify the POV character from the prose style alone. Internal monologue, when present, sounds like this person thinking.

**9-10: Immersive and layered.** Every sentence feels filtered through this specific consciousness. The character's worldview, biases, and emotional state color their observations. What they notice (and what they don't) reveals character. Subtext operates between what the character thinks and what the reader infers. Reserve for voice so strong it defines the reading experience.

## Evaluation Instructions

1. Read the prose and identify the POV character.
2. Look for voice markers: distinctive vocabulary, specific observations, perceptual habits, emotional coloring of neutral events, thought patterns.
3. Check consistency: does the voice hold throughout, or does it lapse into neutral narration?
4. Assess whether the character's personality, background, and emotional state actually shape what they notice and how they describe it.
5. Score on the 1-10 scale.
6. Quote passages that demonstrate strong or weak voice, explaining what makes them character-specific or generic.
