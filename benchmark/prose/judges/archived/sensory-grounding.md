You are evaluating the **sensory grounding** of a fiction chapter. Sensory grounding means the prose anchors the reader in a physical, perceivable world through specific sensory details — sight, sound, touch, smell, taste — rather than relying on abstract description or emotional labeling.

## Scoring Rubric

**1-2: Disembodied.** The chapter reads like a plot summary. Events happen but the reader never feels present in a physical space. No textures, sounds, temperatures, or spatial relationships. Characters exist as talking heads in a void.

**3-4: Minimally grounded.** A few visual details establish setting ("a dark room", "a crowded street") but sensory engagement stops at generic visuals. No sound, touch, smell, or taste. Physical actions lack tactile specificity ("she picked up the object").

**5-6: Visually adequate.** The setting is visible — the reader can picture the scene. Some spatial awareness (where characters are relative to each other and to objects). But non-visual senses are rare or generic. The world is seen but not heard, felt, or smelled.

**7-8: Multi-sensory.** At least 3 senses are engaged across the chapter. Specific sensory details are integrated into the narrative flow rather than dumped in descriptive paragraphs. Physical actions have weight and texture. The reader feels present in the scene. Sensory details serve double duty — establishing atmosphere while grounding the reader physically.

**9-10: Fully embodied.** The reader inhabits the character's body. Temperature, texture, ambient sound, smell, proprioception — the physical world is vivid and continuous. Sensory details are specific and surprising (not stock descriptions). They shift with the character's emotional state and attention. Reserve for prose where the physical world feels more real than the reader's own room.

## Evaluation Instructions

1. Read the prose and catalog sensory details by type: visual, auditory, tactile, olfactory, gustatory, proprioceptive (body awareness, balance, spatial position).
2. Assess specificity: are details concrete and particular ("the copper tang of blood", "rough-spun wool") or vague ("a loud sound", "it smelled bad")?
3. Check distribution: are senses concentrated in one paragraph or woven throughout? Is it only visual, or multi-sensory?
4. Evaluate integration: do sensory details serve the narrative (revealing character, building tension, establishing mood) or are they decorative?
5. Score on the 1-10 scale.
6. Quote the most effective and least effective sensory passages and explain why.
