# Prose Comparison: Qwen3 32B vs Kimi K2

## dark-fantasy

### Qwen3 32B

Istra dipped the quill into the inkwell, the scent of bergamot and iron filling her nostrils. The candlelight flickered across the pages of her journal, its wax pooled like blood under the weight of the candlestick. She added another tally to the column marked 'Cure Administered.' The number 283 stared back at her, taunting in its boldness. Each mark was a life saved, a death evaded. And yet, there was no comfort in it. The ink bled across the paper, as if the journal itself resisted the truth.

Outside, the city of Veyna slept under a shroud of fog, its dying coughs quieted for now. Inside her apothecary, Istra stirred a fresh batch of the cure. The ingredients—root of the moonflower, powdered starbark, and a drop of her own blood—sang together in a bitter harmony. She inhaled deeply, the scent sharp and medicinal, then poured the mixture into a vial labeled with the date and a patient's name: *Aldric's daughter.*

She leaned against the workbench, eyes fixed on the vial. The last time she administered the cure to Aldric's daughter, the child had returned not as her daughter, but as something else. A voice that wasn’t hers had whispered, 'You will obey.' The magistrate had been furious—less because of what the cure did, more because of what it exposed. That night, Istra had buried her journal in the back alley, only to find it the next morning, its pages torn and stained with something that felt like despair.

A sharp knock echoed through the room, jarring her from her thoughts. Istra flinched, her hands tightening around the vial. She had not expected visitors. Not here. Not now. The knock came again, more insistent this time. The fog outside pressed against the windows, thick and suffocating.

She unlatched the door, her breath catching as she faced the figure on the other side. Wren stood there, their small frame trembling slightly in the cold. Their eyes, a shade too bright for a child of their age, met Istra’s with an unsettling calm. Wren held out a letter, the wax seal bearing Aldric's crest.

Istra hesitated before taking the letter. The parchment was cold, the seal unbroken. 'He wants the cure faster,' Wren said, their voice soft but firm. 'He's not waiting for the next batch. He wants results.'

'Send him to hell,' Istra muttered, turning the letter over in her hands. She could feel the weight of its words pressing against her skin, as if the magistrate’s will had already begun to seep into her.

Wren stepped forward, their expression unreadable. 'The ones you healed... something's wrong with them. They speak of places we can't find. They draw maps of things they shouldn't know.'

Istra narrowed her eyes. 'You’re one of them. You should be the last to tell me that.'

Wren nodded slowly. 'I remember things. Places that don't exist. I see them in my drawings. I don't know if it's the cure or something else. But he's watching, Istra. And he's not going to stop.'

The child lingered a moment longer, the fog curling around their boots. Then they vanished into the mist, leaving Istra alone with the letter. She stared at the seal, the wax cracked under her fingers. The magistrate’s demands echoed in her mind, a hollow drumbeat of urgency. Aldric didn’t want to save lives; he wanted control. And he would use the cure to claim it.

Back at her workbench, Istra traced the vial’s surface with a gloved finger. The ink from the letter smudged beneath her touch. She thought of the patient she had just healed—*Aldric's daughter*—and the way the girl’s eyes had shifted, as if something else had taken root. She thought of Wren, their drawings filled with impossible landscapes and voices that weren’t their own. And she thought of the journal, its pages whispering of things she wasn’t ready to face.

The candle had burned low, its flame reduced to a flickering ghost. Istra filled the vial with the cure, her hands steady but her mind racing. She knew what she had to do. She would continue the work, not to save the magistrate’s daughter or appease his demands, but for every name in her journal. For every tally mark. She would find the truth behind the cure—and the cost it demanded. Even if it meant losing her own soul to the bargain. Even if it meant facing the thing that whispered behind the masks and maps and madness.

### Kimi K2

Istra's fingers left crimson prints on the glass vial as she rolled it across her palm. The tincture inside shimmered like liquid copper, catching the lamplight in the cramped apothecary. Three drops left. Three more chances to prove she hadn't damned them all.

She pressed her thumb against the scarred leather of her journal, counting by touch. Forty-six cured in the eastern district. Twenty-three in the merchant quarter. The numbers blurred together like the ink stains she'd left on the parchment—evidence of hands too tired to hold the quill steady. Through the warped window glass, she watched the plague carts rumble past, their drivers wrapped in the same burlap and waxed leather that had become the uniform of the dying city.

The knock made her flinch, sending the vial skittering across the workbench.

"Doctor Istra?" Wren's voice carried through the oak door, too soft for a child who had once screamed himself hoarse with the fever. "You said to come when it happened again."

Istra's spine straightened. She swept the remaining tincture into her apron pocket and unlatched the door. "Show me your tongue."

The boy stepped inside, bringing with him the smell of rain and something else—something sweet and rotten that made her nostrils flare. He opened his mouth without protest, revealing a tongue stained black as pitch. But it was his eyes that made her stomach lurch. They'd gone the color of old parchment, pupils contracted to pinpoints.

"What did you say this time?" She kept her voice clinical, detached.

"The same as before." Wren's fingers worried at the hem of his threadbare tunic. "About the river turning to glass. About the spires that grow from the bones of the dead." He paused, tilted his head like a bird listening to distant music. "She says you're almost ready to listen."

"She?"

"The one who speaks through me. She knows your true name, not the one you wear like borrowed clothes."

Istra's hand found the hilt of her scalpel. The steel felt warm against her palm. "Sit on the stool. I need to examine your eyes."

But Wren remained standing, those parchment eyes fixed on something beyond her shoulder. "She says the cure wasn't meant for flesh alone. She says you've opened a door that was better left closed, and now something else is looking back through your patients."

"Enough." The word cracked like a whip. Istra grabbed the boy's thin shoulders, felt him trembling beneath her grip. "Tell me what you remember from before the fever. Tell me about your mother's face. Your father's voice. Anything real."

For a moment, something flickered across Wren's features—a child's confusion, a child's fear. Then the unnatural calm settled back into place like a mask. "Mother had a mole on her left cheek shaped like a crescent moon. Father's voice cracked when he sang the harvest songs. They died in the first wave, before you found me bleeding from my eyes in the cellar of the orphanage." He smiled, and Istra saw that two of his teeth had turned translucent as glass. "She says gratitude is a poison that tastes like honey."

The scalpel clattered to the floor. Istra backed away until her spine hit the workbench, sending bottles and mortar crashing to the ground. The smell of crushed herbs—wormwood and feverfew—filled the air like incense.

"Leave. Come back tomorrow at dawn."

Wren nodded and shuffled toward the door. His bare feet left wet prints on the stone floor, though rain hadn't fallen in weeks. At the threshold, he paused. "She says when the last drop is gone, you'll finally understand what you've done. She says the city thanks you for its new voice."

The door closed with a whisper. Istra sank to her knees among the broken glass, her fingers finding the vial in her pocket. Three drops. Three drops to hold back an ocean.

She crawled to her journal, flipped to the back pages where she'd recorded the first symptoms of the cure's failure. The handwriting grew progressively erratic, as if someone else had taken control of her hand. Patient twelve speaking in tongues. Patient nineteen drawing symbols in blood. Patient twenty-seven claiming to remember her own death.

The final entry stopped her breath. Written in her script but not her words: *The door is open. The city remembers what it was before we built our stone graves upon its bones. When the last drop falls, we will all remember why we forgot.*

Istra hurled the journal across the room. It struck the wall and fell open, pages fluttering like dying birds. Her reflection stared back from the window glass—gaunt, hollow-eyed, wearing the same expression she saw in her cured patients. How long since she'd looked in a mirror? How long since she'd dared?

She pulled the vial free, held it up to the lamp. The copper liquid inside seemed to pulse with its own heartbeat, keeping time with something vast and patient. Three drops to save a city. Three drops to damn it forever.

Tomorrow she would see Aldric's daughter. The magistrate's only child, wrapped in silk while the poor wrapped their dead in burlap. He would demand the cure, threaten her with the gallows if she withheld it. And she would give it, because the alternative was watching another child die knowing she might have saved them.

The lamp flame guttered, throwing shadows that moved wrong against the walls. In the brief darkness before it steadied, Istra saw something reflected in the window—not her own face, but something wearing it like an ill-fitting mask. Something that smiled with too many teeth.

She threw the vial with a scream of rage and terror. It shattered against the far wall, copper fluid running down the stones like liquid fire. But when she looked down, her palm remained empty. The vile still rested there, intact, patient as a ticking clock.

Three drops. Three chances. Three days until she would have to choose between saving the magistrate's daughter and saving whatever remained of her own soul.

Outside, the plague carts continued their endless circuit, wheels grinding bones to dust beneath the weight of the city's gratitude.

## romance-drama

### Qwen3 32B

Nadia adjusted the pepper mill for the tenth time in fifteen minutes, her hands moving with a precision that belied the restlessness in her chest. The restaurant was nearly empty, just a single couple lingering over the remains of their steak and garlic mussels. The scent of rosemary and charred vegetables lingered in the air, mingling with the salt from the breeze drifting in through the open windows. She was used to silence in the kitchen, but not in the dining room. This was a lull after the storm, the kind that usually brought in clusters of locals eager to talk about the damage and how long it would take to fix. Not tonight.

She wiped her hands on a dish towel and glanced at the window. Beyond it, the sea looked restless, the waves tugging at the shore with an almost deliberate persistence. It was as if the town itself was holding its breath. Her divorce was final now, and the thought should have felt like freedom. Instead, it felt like a door closing behind her. She pushed it from her mind and returned to the open window of the walk-in fridge, where her latest experimental dish—a fusion of the local seafood and bold, Eastern spices—was cooling.

The bell on her front door jingled, and she turned to see Jem from next door leaning against the counter, his usual loud entrance tempered by something more subdued. He wore his typical work clothes—jeans and a faded fisherman's cap—but his eyes were sharp with concern. 

"You seen the damage?" he asked, voice low, as if the walls themselves might overhear. 

"I saw the water rising last night, but that's it," she replied, setting a plate of her dish on the counter. "I thought the storm was just going to be a nuisance. Not a full-on flood." 

Jem shook his head and tossed a rolled-up newspaper onto the counter. "You're not going to believe this. The storm washed out the east wall of my restaurant. The whole building, it's a write-off. They won't be paying out for three months, maybe more. I'm on borrowed time as it is." 

Nadia unfolded the newspaper and scanned the headlines, the words blurring before she could focus. "The damage is worse than I thought," she read aloud, the dread settling in her chest like a stone. She looked up at Jem, who had already turned away, his hands gripping the counter as if bracing against the weight of the conversation.

"It's not just my place," he added quietly. "They're going to need your kitchen to keep things running until the insurance kicks in. If it kicks in. I heard Vera was already in town, talking to the insurance adjuster. She’s been circling for months, and this storm just made it easier for her to step in." 

Nadia folded the newspaper and placed it back on the counter. Her mind raced with calculations—how long she could afford to let her own restaurant down for the next three months, how many of her loyal customers would turn to Jem's place instead, and whether the town would even need both restaurants to survive. She thought of Vera's polite smile and the way she always had an offer ready, no matter how unreasonable it seemed. A plan. Always a plan.

Back in the kitchen, she moved as if possessed by a need to control something, anything. She chopped, stirred, and tasted a sauce that was too sharp with lime and not enough heat. The flavors were off, but she didn’t care. She needed to make something work, to create something that could not be bought or sold. As she worked, she thought of the walls of Jem's kitchen—how they were now gone, how the space they had both carved out for themselves was suddenly vulnerable. But there was no time for hesitation. She turned off the burner beneath the sauce and wiped her hands again. There would be no room for fear or doubt in what came next. She would find a way to protect what was hers, and perhaps, in the process, find something new.

### Kimi K2

The blade kissed steel as Nadia's knife reduced shallots to translucent moons. Her breath misted in the kitchen's chill, mixing with the scent of last night's bouillabaisse that still clung to the vents. Through the service window, the dining room sat empty—white tablecloths pressed, wine glasses gleaming, chairs pushed in perfect alignment. The sea visible through the windows looked flat as polished pewter.

She set down her knife and rubbed her palms against her apron. Flour dust puffed from yesterday's baking, ghosting across black cotton. Four years of mornings like this—first in the door, last to leave, inventory lists scrolling behind her eyelids while she slept. Since the divorce, she'd doubled down on perfect mise en place, perfect profit margins, perfect everything. The silence in the dining room felt heavier than usual, as if the tables themselves were holding their breath.

A metal clatter shattered the quiet. Nadia's pulse spiked before she recognized it—someone hammering the back door. She wiped her hands and crossed through the prep area, her footsteps echoing on the recently mopped floor. The door's deadbolt stuck as it always did when the salt air swelled.

Jem stood on the stoop, his orange slicker dripping onto the concrete, hair plastered dark against his skull. He held two mugs of coffee, steam swirling in the cold morning. Behind him, gulls wheeled and cried over the harbor.

"You're early," Nadia said, not stepping aside. "Kitchen's not open for three hours."

"Saw your lights." Jem thrust a mug toward her. "Dark roast, two sugars. You look like you swallowed nightshade."

She took the coffee despite herself. The ceramic burned through her palm. "Long night."

"Longer morning." Jem's grin carried no warmth. "Vera was at the wharf when the fishing boats came in. Said she's got investors coming tomorrow. Wants to show them the property potential before tourist season hits."

Nadia's hand tightened around her mug. "She can't force a sale."

"Maybe. Maybe not." Jem stepped closer, rainwater dripping from his sleeves. "But she's got that storm damage report now. Says your roof quote was twenty grand more than mine."

"Because I actually got a licensed contractor."

"And because you used the expensive materials." Jem's eyes narrowed. "Don't make you better, just makes you poorer."

Heat flushed up Nadia's spine. She'd built The Meridian from nothing—every copper pot, every imported spice, every piece of hand-blown glassware earned through sixteen-hour days and careful reinvestment. Jem's Crab Shack had inherited its weathered charm, its broken-in booths, its customers who came more for the owner's jokes than the frozen breaded shrimp.

"What do you want, Jem?"

"Same thing you want. To keep Vera from buying our buildings out from under us."

The wind shifted, carrying the smell of diesel and ocean rot. Nadia stepped back into the kitchen, holding the door. Jem followed, his boots squelching on the clean floor. He surveyed the gleaming range, the ordered racks of pans, the precise arrangement of utensils.

"Place looks like a museum."

"It's called professional standards."

"It's called having your head up your—"

"Finish that sentence and you're back on the street."

Jem shrugged, but his jaw tightened. "Look, Vera's squeezing both of us. Insurance is dragging their feet on payouts, she knows it, and she's got time to wait us out. But maybe we got something she doesn't."

"Which is?"

"Two restaurants on prime waterfront. Together, we're worth more than either of us alone." He set his coffee on the prep counter, ignoring her sharp inhale. "My dining room took four feet of water. Your kitchen's intact. My kitchen's fine, your dining room's fine. We work together for three months, we both stay afloat."

Nadia stared at him. Outside, a buoy clanged against the morning quiet. She could hear her own heartbeat in her ears. "Share facilities."

"Share everything. Recipes, suppliers, customers. Split the profits down the middle."

"Your customers wouldn't know duck confit if it bit them."

"Your customers wouldn't know a decent lobster roll if it—"

*My grandmother's recipe won six years running at the festival.*

"And my sous chef won Paris Young Chef of the Year."

"Congratulations. Now he's cooking for tourists who want ketchup with their filet mignon."

Nadia set her coffee down before she threw it. The mug's base clicked against stainless steel loud as a gunshot. "Get out."

Jem's shoulders rose and fell. He reached for his coffee, reconsidered. "Vera's coming tomorrow at ten. You want to sink alone, that's your choice."

The door slammed behind him hard enough to rattle plates on the wall rack. Nadia stood motionless, staring at the space where he'd been. She could still smell his damp slicker—salt and old fish and something like cedar smoke.

She moved through the kitchen automatically, checking temperatures, rotating stock. Her knife work resumed, carrots surrendering to perfect julienne. But her hands shook now, each cut fractionally off. She set the knife down and gripped the counter's edge until her knuckles whitened.

They couldn't. They absolutely could not share space. The thought of Jem's muddy boots on her clean floor, his loud laughter during service, his casual approach to food safety. She'd spent four years proving she didn't need anyone—certainly not a man who thought cooking meant drowning everything in Old Bay.

But the silence pressed against her eardrums. Tomorrow Vera would come with her clipboard and her patient smile and her numbers that always added up in her favor. The insurance company would stall. The bills would mount. And Nadia would watch everything she'd built disappear into someone else's development plan.

The carrots waited like soldiers on the cutting board. She picked up her knife, set it down again. Through the service window, she could see Jem's place across the parking lot—paint peeling, sign faded by salt spray, but full every night with locals who called him by name and left tips in the jar for his kid's college fund.

She'd worked too hard to fail now. But she couldn't survive alone. And Jem, for all his faults, was drowning just as surely as she was.

The knife gleamed under the fluorescent light. Tomorrow she'd have to decide. But tonight, she had prep lists and orders and staff schedules, and the comfort of routine that kept the world at bay. She reached for the carrots and began to cut—perfect, precise, measured.

Outside, the harbor lights flickered on one by one, reflecting on water that looked deceptively calm.
